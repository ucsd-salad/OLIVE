Looking at this scenario: A two-year-old male is rushed to the clinic acutely convulsing.

**Medical Analysis:**
- A convulsing child would be unresponsive or only responsive to pain on the AVPU scale
- Convulsions indicate a neurological emergency
- This falls under "C" in ABCD (Circulation, **Coma**, **Convulsion**)
- A child who is actively convulsing would be assessed as AVPU = Pain or Unresponsive

Since the child is "acutely convulsing," they would be at most responsive to pain (AVPU.Pain), which triggers the `CirculationComaConulsion` check, which feeds into `Emergency`. This should result in **Emergency** triage.

The child is 2 years old = 24 months.

## Triage Assessment: Two-Year-Old with Acute Convulsions

**This child should be triaged as an EMERGENCY.**

### Explanation

Based on the verified assessment, a two-year-old (24 months) presenting with acute convulsions is classified as **Emergency** triage. Here's why:

The triage system follows the **ABCD** approach:

- **A (Airway):** Clear — the child is breathing, no airway obstruction, no central cyanosis.
- **B (Breathing):** No severe respiratory distress — no chest indrawing, no accessory muscle use, able to feed, no very fast breathing.
- **C (Circulation, Coma, Convulsion):** **This is where the emergency is triggered.** A child who is actively convulsing is assessed on the AVPU scale as responsive only to **Pain** (not alert, not responding to verbal stimuli). Under the ABCD protocol, a patient who is only responsive to pain — indicating coma or active convulsion — meets the criteria for emergency triage.
- **D (Dehydration):** No signs — no sunken eyes, normal skin turgor.

### What This Means

Even though the child's airway, breathing, circulation, and hydration status are otherwise stable, the convulsion alone — captured by the AVPU assessment showing the child is only responsive to pain — is sufficient to classify this as an **Emergency** under the "C" (Circulation, Coma, Convulsion) criterion.

### Immediate Action

As an Emergency triage case, this child requires **immediate** assessment and treatment ahead of all Priority and Queue patients. Per emergency protocols, you should:

1. Manage the airway and ensure the child is in a safe position
2. Administer appropriate anticonvulsant therapy
3. Assess for underlying causes (e.g., fever/malaria, meningitis, poisoning, head injury)
4. Continuously monitor vital signs

**Do not delay treatment** — emergency-triaged patients must receive care without waiting.
